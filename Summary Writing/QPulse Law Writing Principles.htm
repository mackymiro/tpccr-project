Q-Pulse Law - Content Management Principles 

 

Content areas to be written are: 

1.     Legislative Summaries 

2.     Change Comments - following any new additions & amendments to Summaries

 

Writing Principles: 

o   Content should be clear, direct and in laymans terms how would you describe it to someone you have only just met? 

o   Ensure the content is written in your own words i.e. with no copying of original text 

o   You may need to research local terminology/language for the terms used in the legislation 

o   Use local, meaningful language, considering the jurisdiction you are writing for 

o   Use sub-headings where it helps to usefully break up the content 

 

Legislative Summaries 

 

These hold the legislative information that sits behind/backs up the checklists. 

 

This should give the reader the highlights of the legislation. The intention is not to recreate a long complex document as the reader can get to the full text, but they need to get a sense of each relevant section so they can decide whether to read deeper. 

 

Make a note of tangible things that a client may need to do in order to demonstrate that they are compliant:

e.g. 

          Obtain a licence/registration/approval 

          Notify someone 

          Keep a record 

          Submit a report 

          Get an expert to check something (equipment safety, health, air quality, water samples, etc) 

          Carry out a risk assessment 

          Complete a manifest/transport document 

          Carry out training 

          Post notices/posters 

          Deadlines 

          Monetary obligations 

 

We do not need to note what the regulators or government bodies must do, unless for information/reference due to a subsequent client implication e.g. government reporting which may/will require industry input in future.

 

We do not need to include numerical reference changes, or minor text corrections, unless they are fundamental to the understanding of the legislation (e/g/ leading to a change in definition).

Writing the summary text: 

Title 

o   Ensure the title reflects the naming convention for the different legislation types/levels as set out in the Country Profile e.g.  decree, decision, regulation, order 

o   Title should be taken from the legislation and where translated, led by the countrys language/terminology 

 

Link 

o   At the top of this section, provide the link to the original source material (official legislation source). It should start with Link to full text >> 

o   Name the hyperlink with the Year & Number, as provided on the source e.g. 2019 c. 18 (this may change by jurisdiction  look for an equivalent) 

 

Purpose & Scope 

 

All summaries should include Purpose & Scope as an initial sub-heading, which should then be followed with a short description: 

o   what does the legislation do? 

o   why does it do it? 

o   who needs to adhere to it? 

o   who/what are the exceptions?

o   who regulates it? (but use with caution  generally only where the legislation expressly makes reference to the body) 

o   summary of possible consequences of non-compliance 

 

It is often useful to include a definition of the key focus of the legislation, to ensure the reader knows what it is about 

 

Continued Legislative Summary 

Once the Purpose & Scope is defined, describe any other key elements of the legislation, including anything which requires more detail. Further sub-headings may be useful for the additional content but may not be needed. 

 

Commencement Orders - details of these are added to the Act summary itself, and the dates and link listed in the Timetable section of the Content Manager. 

 

Note: An Act (or equivalent primary level piece) does not necessarily come into effect immediately, typically subsequent legislation will define when all of it, or just sections of it, come into effect. 

 

If published, we will include a piece of legislation which is not yet in force, as it gives clients advance notice of their obligations. However, we would ensure the summary and change comment clearly state when it comes into force.

 

 

Change Comments 

Following any new content additions &/or amendments, we need to draft a 1 or 2 sentence description of the addition/amendment. 

 

Where it concerns an amendment, it should state the name of the amending and amended legislations and provide any context for the change.

 

Note: To borrow a phrase from investment, this is your so-called Elevator Pitch what you would tell someone about the change if you had 30 seconds with them in an Elevator between floors. 

 

Always look to add to the Change Comment who it is relevant for e.g. Important change for manufacturers or Applicable to those industries required to report on greenhouse gas emissions as this will help clients when they read the change summary email. In broader, more general pieces of legislation, this may not always be appropriate.  

 

Translations 

When a jurisdiction requires an alternative language, as well as English (this could be the local language or a defined company language), the following elements are translated: 

         Legislation title 

         Keywords 

 

 

Appendix 1 Guidance for when reviewing topics for relevance 

 

Not Relevant

Nothing more local than region in terms of jurisdiction e.g. state not city

 

Official legislation or standards only not locally produced policies.

 

No proposed or draft legislation, or EU decisions on approach to be taken.

 

Specifics - nothing too geographically specific, (e.g. Dogger Bank Creyke Beck Offshore Wind Farm (Amendment) Order 2019) or technically very specific (i.e. technical specifications for an industry, or formulae)

 

Fishing  We do have some safety-related legislation in UK and Ireland but generally dont cover this. (Marine/Coastal conservation which limits fishing is a separate topic and is relevant).

 

 

Review further for relevance

 

Aviation not all relevant, but elements such as transport of dangerous goods, environmental impact and occupational safety would be.

 

Agriculture  we would include things like oil storage tanks and land-spreading of wastewater sludge, and intensive farming (large number of animals) typically requires an environmental permit and environmental impact assessment (EIA). 

 

Drugs we cover poison, drugs and explosives precursors.

 

Electricity/Energy-related topics gauge relevance from safety / environmental perspective e.g. incentives for green energy, safety of networks etc. 

 

Firearms not personal use or armed forces - only when it relates to business use/access (safety and security) in a particular jurisdiction we often have a checklist on Violence at Work to cover drivers and lone workers (e.g. security or facilities staff). 

 

Food - unless related to general canteen/on-site restaurant (HACCP management system) include some specifics of food safety legislation.

 

Marine - limited to ship and offshore (oil rigs, wind farms, undersea cables) pollution and general ship safety.

 

Medical products  would only include medical devices and radiation instruments.

 

Mining - where it is health, safety or environment based

 

Planning sometimes relevant look at whether the detail impacts on safety or environmental elements.

 

Plant health/Phytosanitary only include if related to international regulations around wooden packaging, pest controls & controlling timber products (wood products only) or nature conservation & biodiversity 

 

Trapping/Hunting - we cover protection of animals in general from an environmental perspective e.g. trading in protected animals (products) and protected species/habitats (birds, bats, whales, etc).
