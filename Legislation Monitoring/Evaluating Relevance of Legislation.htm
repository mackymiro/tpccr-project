Evaluating the Relevance of Legislation

A.      Check the list of topics and sub-topics provided by the client.

 

         We are looking for legislation related to health, safety, and environment, specifically legislation dealing with the matters listed as topics and sub-topics. Accordingly, we can use the topics and sub-topics as keywords to help assess the relevance of a particular legislation. However, do not rely solely on the keywords because the topics and sub-topics are general terms which may cover a myriad of related terms.

 

B.      Consider the following questions to gauge relevance:

 

1.       Does the legislation provide for regulations relating to/covering/affecting the topics and sub-topics in the context of environment, health, and safety?

2.       Are the topics and sub-topics the subject matter of the legislation which impacts environment, health, and safety?

3.       Does the legislation impose duties/obligations on persons, grant powers to regulators, establish offices etc. relating to/affecting the topics and sub-topics for the maintenance of the environment, health, and safety?

 

C.      Examine and analyze all the following key elements (if applicable).

 

         In order to answer the questions, we must analyze the different parts of the legislation. Reading the entire text of the legislation is not required to evaluate relevance. Just scan all the key elements of the legislation that will give an idea as to its subject matter and purpose. Because we are dealing with different types of legislation from various jurisdictions, they may not contain the same parts or elements. Thus, it is good to note that most of the key information required to determine relevance are found either in the beginning or end part of the legislation (where analogous elements may also be found).

 

1.       Table of Contents this outlines the contents of the legislation.

 

2.       Short Title this expresses the general subject covered by the legislation, and all provisions of the legislation are connected to that subject; thus, the title is a good indicator of whether the legislation is appropriate or not.

 

3.       Long Title this sets out the subject, scope, and purpose of the legislation.

 

 

4.       Headings legislation is divided into different parts or sections with each subdivision identified by a heading or subheading that gives an idea as to the matters discussed in the particular part.

 

 

5.       Schedules some legislation have documents annexed at the end called schedules that add more details to the main text or provide examples of forms or rules to be used under the legislation.

 

 

6.       Explanatory Notes (found under the "Explanatory Notes" or "More Resources" tab) text created by the government department responsible for the subject matter of the legislation to explain what the legislation sets out to achieve. Legislation may also be accompanied by an Explanatory Memorandum, Executive Note, or Policy Note.

 

 

 

 

Appendix 1 Guidance for when reviewing topics for relevance 

 

Not Relevant

Nothing more local than region in terms of jurisdiction e.g. state not city

 

Official legislation or standards only not locally produced policies.

 

No proposed or draft legislation, or EU decisions on approach to be taken.

 

Specifics - nothing too geographically specific, (e.g. Dogger Bank Creyke Beck Offshore Wind Farm (Amendment) Order 2019) or technically very specific (i.e. technical specifications for an industry, or formulae)

 

Fishing  We do have some safety-related legislation in UK and Ireland but generally dont cover this. (Marine/Coastal conservation which limits fishing is a separate topic and is relevant).

 

 

Review further for relevance

 

Aviation not all relevant, but elements such as transport of dangerous goods, environmental impact and occupational safety would be.

 

Agriculture  we would include things like oil storage tanks and land-spreading of wastewater sludge, and intensive farming (large number of animals) typically requires an environmental permit and environmental impact assessment (EIA). 

 

Drugs we cover poison, drugs and explosives precursors.

 

Electricity/Energy-related topics gauge relevance from safety / environmental perspective e.g. incentives for green energy, safety of networks etc. 

 

Firearms not personal use or armed forces - only when it relates to business use/access (safety and security) in a particular jurisdiction we often have a checklist on Violence at Work to cover drivers and lone workers (e.g. security or facilities staff). 

 

Food - unless related to general canteen/on-site restaurant (HACCP management system) include some specifics of food safety legislation.

 

Marine - limited to ship and offshore (oil rigs, wind farms, undersea cables) pollution and general ship safety.

 

Medical products  would only include medical devices and radiation instruments.

 

Mining - where it is health, safety or environment based

 

Planning sometimes relevant look at whether the detail impacts on safety or environmental elements.

 

Plant health/Phytosanitary only include if related to international regulations around wooden packaging, pest controls & controlling timber products (wood products only) or nature conservation & biodiversity 

 

Trapping/Hunting - we cover protection of animals in general from an environmental perspective e.g. trading in protected animals (products) and protected species/habitats (birds, bats, whales, etc).
